What is the Heart? (Wikipedia)

The heart is a muscular organ in most animals, which pumps blood through the blood vessels of the circulatory system. The pumped blood carries oxygen and nutrients to the body, while carrying metabolic waste such as carbon dioxide to the lungs. In humans, the heart is approximately the size of a closed fist and is located between the lungs, in the middle compartment of the chest.

Did you know? The human heart beats more than 2.5 billion times during an average lifetime!

Heart Anatomy (Wikipedia)

Four Chambers

  • Right Atrium – Receives deoxygenated blood from the body through the superior and inferior vena cava.
  • Right Ventricle – Pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery.
  • Left Atrium – Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs through the pulmonary veins.
  • Left Ventricle – Pumps oxygenated blood to the entire body via the aorta (thickest chamber wall).

Heart Valves

  • Tricuspid Valve – Between right atrium and right ventricle
  • Pulmonary Valve – Between right ventricle and pulmonary artery
  • Mitral (Bicuspid) Valve – Between left atrium and left ventricle
  • Aortic Valve – Between left ventricle and aorta

Layers of Heart Wall

  • Endocardium – Inner layer lining chambers and valves
  • Myocardium – Middle muscular layer responsible for pumping
  • Epicardium – Outer protective layer
  • Pericardium – Double-walled sac containing the heart

Blood Flow Through Heart

Body → Right Atrium → Right Ventricle → Lungs (O₂ pickup) → Left Atrium → Left Ventricle → Body

Valves ensure one-way blood flow and prevent backflow.

Cardiac Cycle & Function

Cardiac Cycle Phases

  • Systole – Contraction phase: Ventricles contract, pumping blood to lungs and body
  • Diastole – Relaxation phase: Chambers fill with blood

Electrical Conduction System

  • SA Node (Sinoatrial Node) – Natural pacemaker, initiates heartbeat (60-100 bpm)
  • AV Node (Atrioventricular Node) – Delays signal, allowing ventricles to fill
  • Bundle of His – Conducts signal to ventricles
  • Purkinje Fibers – Spread signal throughout ventricular walls
ECG/EKG: Electrocardiogram records the heart's electrical activity through P wave, QRS complex, and T wave.

Cardiac Output

Cardiac Output = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume

Average resting cardiac output: ~5 liters/minute. During exercise, it can increase 4-7 times!

Common Heart Disorders (Wikipedia)

  • Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) Plaque buildup in coronary arteries reduces blood flow to heart muscle. Leading cause of heart attacks.
  • Arrhythmia Irregular heartbeat - too fast (tachycardia), too slow (bradycardia), or chaotic (fibrillation).
  • Heart Failure Heart cannot pump enough blood to meet body's needs. Not a sudden stop, but gradual weakening.
  • Valvular Heart Disease Damage or defect in one of the four heart valves (stenosis or regurgitation).
  • Congenital Heart Defects Structural problems present at birth (e.g., septal defects, tetralogy of Fallot).
  • Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack) Blood flow blockage causes death of heart muscle tissue.
Prevention Tips: Regular exercise, healthy diet (low saturated fat), no smoking, stress management, regular checkups.
